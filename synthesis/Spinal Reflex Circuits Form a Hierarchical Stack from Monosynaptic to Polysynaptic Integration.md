# Spinal Reflex Circuits Form a Hierarchical Stack from Monosynaptic to Polysynaptic Integration

## Insight
The spinal cord implements at least three distinct levels of sensorimotor integration via reflex arcs of increasing synaptic complexity — all operating below cortical awareness:

1. **Monosynaptic (myotatic/stretch reflex):** Ia afferent directly onto alpha motor neuron. Fastest feedback loop for maintaining muscle length; the basis of the clinical deep tendon reflex.
2. **Disynaptic (Golgi tendon organ reflex):** Ib afferent through an inhibitory interneuron, inhibiting the agonist and exciting the antagonist. Protective against excessive force generation.
3. **Polysynaptic (flexor withdrawal + crossed extension):** Nociceptive afferent through multiple propriospinal interneurons producing bilateral coordinated response — ipsilateral withdrawal and contralateral postural support.

## Evidence
Consistently described across NEURO 302 and 404 spinal cord lectures. Reciprocal inhibition via Ia inhibitory interneuron (disynaptic), Golgi tendon organ reflex (disynaptic), and crossed extension via propriospinal crossing interneurons (polysynaptic) are all documented in the same spinal cord unit across multiple source notes.

## Implications
The spinal cord is not merely a conduit for supraspinal commands — it is an active, layered sensorimotor processing system. After spinal cord injury above these circuits, reflexes can persist or become hyperactive (UMN spasticity, exaggerated deep tendon reflexes); below the injury, reflexes are absent (LMN flaccidity). This hierarchy directly explains the UMN vs LMN lesion sign distinction — a core clinical neurology diagnostic framework.

## Applicability
Core to NEURO 302 and NEURO 404. Directly applicable to clinical neurology (UMN/LMN distinction), spinal cord injury assessment, rehabilitation science, and pharmacological targets for spasticity.
