# Cortical Inhibitory Interneurons Implement Spatial Division of Control Over Pyramidal Cell Excitability

## Insight
Three distinct inhibitory interneuron subtypes — basket cells, chandelier (axo-axonic) cells, and Martinotti cells — each target a different anatomical compartment of pyramidal neurons. Basket cells contact proximal dendrites, chandelier cells synapse at the axon hillock (spike initiation zone), and Martinotti cells contact distal dendritic tufts. This spatial division means inhibitory control over pyramidal output is implemented at three independent anatomical checkpoints, each with distinct functional leverage over dendritic integration versus action potential generation.

## Evidence
Covered in NEURO 302 lecture on cortical neurons (2026-01-30). All three interneuron subtypes explicitly enumerated with their synaptic targets in a single lecture. Chandelier cell axon hillock targeting flagged as "optimal control point for synapse speed," indicating the functional significance of spatial targeting was explicitly taught.

## Implications
Damage to or dysfunction in different interneuron subtypes will produce distinct patterns of pyramidal hyperexcitability. Chandelier cell loss removes control at the spike initiation point — uncontrolled action potential generation. Basket cell loss disrupts proximal dendritic integration — altered summation of inputs near the soma. Martinotti cell loss disinhibits distal dendritic tufts — altered integration of long-range corticocortical inputs. These distinctions may explain why specific interneuron subpopulations are selectively lost in different forms of focal epilepsy and cortical dysrhythmia.

## Applicability
Cortical neurophysiology (NEURO 302); relevant to epilepsy mechanism, cortical circuit disorders, schizophrenia (where parvalbumin interneurons including chandelier cells are implicated), and pharmacological targeting of inhibitory circuits.
