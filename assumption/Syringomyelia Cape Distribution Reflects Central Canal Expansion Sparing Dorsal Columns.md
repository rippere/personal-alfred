# Syringomyelia Cape Distribution Reflects Central Canal Expansion Sparing Dorsal Columns

## Claim
Syringomyelia — a fluid-filled cyst (syrinx) expanding within the spinal cord — produces a characteristic "cape" distribution of sensory loss: bilateral loss of pain and temperature sensation at and around the lesion level, with initial preservation of fine touch and proprioception. This pattern reflects the central location of the syrinx, which damages the decussating spinothalamic fibers while sparing the peripherally situated dorsal columns.

## Basis
The spinothalamic tract (pain/temperature) decussates in the anterior commissure near the central canal, making it the first casualty of centrally expanding pathology. The dorsal columns (fine touch, proprioception) run peripherally and are spared until the syrinx expands considerably. The anterior horn is also affected, producing ipsilateral weakness, atrophy, and fasciculations at the lesion level.

## Evidence Trail
- 2026-02-01 (approx): Covered in spinal cord syndromes lecture. Cape distribution identified as the distinguishing clinical pattern. Common syrinx locations: T5-6 or C4-5 following vertebral fracture.

## Impact
Cape distribution is a clinical diagnostic indicator for syringomyelia. Contrasts with Brown-Sequard syndrome (lateral hemisection, which produces crossed deficits) — together these two syndromes demonstrate how lesion geometry within the cord maps onto specific sensory/motor deficit patterns, a key clinical reasoning framework.
