# Crossed Extension Reflex Maintains Bilateral Postural Stability During Nociceptive Withdrawal

## Claim
When a painful stimulus triggers ipsilateral limb flexion (withdrawal reflex), a simultaneous crossed extension reflex activates contralateral limb extensors. This coupling ensures that as one limb withdraws from danger, the opposite limb extends to bear the body's weight, preventing postural collapse.

## Basis
Spinal reflex circuits are organized to produce coordinated bilateral responses to unilateral inputs. The crossed extension component requires propriospinal interneurons crossing the midline — a polysynaptic circuit distinct from the monosynaptic myotatic reflex and the disynaptic Golgi tendon organ reflex.

## Evidence Trail
- Covered across multiple NEURO 302 and NEURO 404 lectures on spinal cord reflexes.
- Described explicitly: nociceptive input produces ipsilateral limb flexion plus contralateral limb extension.

## Impact
Demonstrates that the spinal cord implements sensorimotor integration beyond simple stimulus-response — bilateral postural coordination occurs without cortical involvement. Relevant to understanding locomotion control, clinical spasticity patterns after spinal cord injury, and what functional capabilities are preserved when supraspinal input is lost.
